Phrase and FormWe first talked about the idea of phrase earlier, when we introduced the concept of ‘grouping’.

When a musician is said to perform very expressively, it usually means that they are playing in a manner that shows the audience how well the performer has understood the structure and design of a song or composition.

They are communicating the connections among groups of notes, grouping or phrasing them in a way that somehow makes sense and sounds ‘right’ for that particular musical style or tradition.

An anacrusis describes the same thing as the 'pickup' or the 'pickup notes'.

You can think of it as a partial, incomplete bar just before the strong downbeat of the first full, complete bar.

Repeat MarksThis is a good moment to learn about another symbol used in stave notation: repeat marks.

We don’t discuss repeat marks in this week’s videos, but they’re very useful for indicating something that affects the structure and form of a musical composition.

Repeat marks indicate a particular section of notation that should be played more than once.

When reading music, if you encounter this sign, it means “Go back to the start, and play the same music again”:Once you reach the symbol again (for the second time), you continue on past it to the next section of the score.If you encounter a corresponding sign facing the opposite way, it indicates the beginning of a section that you are going to be asked to repeat:So, in the following example, all the music contained within the repeat marks should be repeated:After the section has been repeated once, the player would simply ignore the repeat mark and continue on through the music in order to avoid an infinite loop.When a repeated section is almost the same but needs to end differently the second time around, number brackets above the bars will indicate this.
